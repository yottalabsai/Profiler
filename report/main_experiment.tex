\section{Evaluation}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}

\textbf{Hardware.}  All experiments run on a single GPU:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=1.5em]
  \item \textbf{NVIDIA A100-SXM4-80GB}: 108 SMs, 80\,GB HBM2e (2\,TB/s memory bandwidth),
    3rd-generation Tensor Cores (HMMA BF16/TF32), peak BF16 throughput 312\,TFLOPS,
    Ampere architecture (sm80).
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Software.}  PyTorch 2.11; CUDA 12.8; Nsight Systems 2024.6; Nsight Compute 2025.1;
Python 3.11.

\textbf{Measurement protocol.}  Attributed kernel times are measured from \nsys CUPTI records
(GPU-domain wall clock), with 3--5 warm-up iterations and 10 measured iterations per workload.
\ncu replay is performed in application mode (\texttt{--replay-mode application}), collecting all
20 metrics in 4--8 passes; absolute replay durations are 2--5$\times$ inflated relative to real
wall clock and are used only for relative before/after comparisons.  Layer deduplication is
enabled for models with repeated layer structure (e.g.\ GPT-2), profiling only unique
representatives and propagating metrics to duplicates.

\textbf{Baselines.}  We compare three configurations:
\begin{enumerate*}[label=(\arabic*)]
  \item \emph{Eager FP32}: standard PyTorch inference with \texttt{torch.compile(backend="inductor")}
    and default settings, FP32 precision (Inductor FP32 baseline);
  \item \emph{Optimized}: workload-specific backend applying BF16 promotion and other
    hardware-evidence-motivated patterns.
\end{enumerate*}
Eager-mode FP32 (without \texttt{torch.compile}) serves as a secondary reference for the speedup
table; Inductor FP32 vs.\ optimized comparisons are pending for all workloads
(\textbf{[TBD]} in \tabref{tab:speedup}).

\subsection{Hardware Counter Validation}

\tabref{tab:hwcounters} shows hardware counter values before and after optimization for GPT-2,
demonstrating systematic bottleneck elimination.  All values are from \ncu application-mode replay
($^\dagger$).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\caption{Key hardware counter values for GPT-2 on A100-SXM4-80GB ($^\dagger$, ncu replay).
  GEMM counters are duration-weighted aggregates (\equref{eq:dwmean}) across \texttt{aten::mm}
  and \texttt{aten::addmm}; FMHA counters are per-call values for
  \texttt{aten::\_efficient\_attention\_forward}.}
\label{tab:hwcounters}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrl}
\toprule
\textbf{Counter} & \textbf{Baseline} & \textbf{Optimized} & \textbf{Diagnosed Cause} \\
\midrule
TC active, mm/addmm (\%)    & 0.0\%            & 55.9\%   & FP32 SIMT $\to$ BF16 HMMA (\texttt{s16816gemm}) \\
Registers/thread (fmha)     & 168              & 128      & BF16 fmha uses fewer per-thread registers \\
Local mem.\ spills (fmha)   & 24,468,480\,B    & 0\,B     & BF16 fmha keeps attn.\ matrix on-chip \\
Achieved occupancy (fmha)   & 6.30\%           & 6.34\%   & Wave starvation persists (96 CTAs / 108 SMs) \\
Waves/SM (attention)        & 0.89             & 0.89     & B=4, 12 heads: below 1-wave threshold \\
FFN up-proj per-kernel      & 197,952\,ns$^\dagger$ & 21,120\,ns$^\dagger$ & 9.4$\times$ (BF16 128$\times$128 tile, 3-stage pipe) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{table}

BF16 promotion drives the GEMM speedup by activating Tensor Core HMMA instructions (TC: 0\%
$\to$ 55.9\% on \texttt{aten::mm} and \texttt{aten::addmm}).  The attention speedup (1.79$\times$)
is explained by register spill elimination: switching to \texttt{fmha\_cutlassF\_bf16\_aligned}
reduces per-thread registers from 168 to 128 and drops 24.5\,MB of local-memory spills to zero.
Achieved occupancy on the fmha kernel improves only marginally (6.30\% $\to$ 6.34\%) because
wave starvation is structural at B=4 (96 CTAs across 108 SMs = 0.89 waves); eliminating it
requires either B$\ge$16 or a Flash Attention~v2 kernel that processes multiple heads per SM.

\subsection{MLP Activations}

The MLP workload is expected to highlight activation-function bottlenecks that are invisible to
timing-only profilers.  The baseline profile is expected to reveal low \texttt{ipc\_active} on the
\texttt{tanh} activation kernel, caused by SFU pipeline serialization: the \texttt{\_\_tanhf}
special function routes through a 4-cycle-per-instruction SFU pipeline, stalling all other warps.
BF16 promotion is expected to activate Tensor Core paths on all four linear layers
(\texttt{tensor\_core\_active\_pct}: $0\% \to$ significant), and replacing \texttt{tanh} with
\texttt{gelu(approximate=`tanh')} in the FFN context is expected to eliminate the SFU stall.

\subsection{ConvBlock}

The ConvBlock workload on A100-SXM4-80GB illustrates non-GEMM bottlenecks.  Two bottlenecks
dominate the baseline profile (total attributed time: 4,119,109\,ns, 0\% unattributed): (1)
\texttt{aten::\_native\_batch\_norm\_legit\_no\_training} (985,505\,ns, 23.9\% of baseline)
eliminated by folding BatchNorm running statistics into the preceding Conv2d weights before
\texttt{torch.compile}; (2) \texttt{convertTensor\_kernel} launches on every forward pass caused
by cuDNN NCHW-to-NHWC coercion for FP32 inputs, eliminated entirely by
\texttt{model.to(memory\_format=torch.channels\_last)}.  BF16 promotion activates the cuDNN NHWC
BF16 kernel path and routes the \texttt{addmm} Linear head to BF16 cuBLAS (TC: 0\% $\to$ 2.56\%,
2.90$\times$ speedup on the head).  Kernel launches reduced by 22\% (273 $\to$ 214).

Measured speedup: \textbf{2.93$\times$} (4,119,109\,ns $\to$ 1,404,657\,ns attributed time).

\subsection{LSTM Sequence Encoder}

The LSTM workload is expected to reveal a distinct attribution pattern.  Under
PyTorch~2.11 + \texttt{torch.compile(backend="inductor")},
\texttt{nn.LSTM} dispatches to \texttt{aten::\_cudnn\_rnn}, a single opaque cuDNN operator.
Dynamo produces \emph{zero} traceable FX subgraphs for \texttt{nn.LSTM}: the operator enters
via \texttt{torch.\_VF.lstm}, a C extension entry point that Dynamo cannot graph-capture.
All pre-compile modifications and FX pattern rewrites are therefore inapplicable and degrade
gracefully.

Hardware counter attribution is expected to be high UNATTRIBUTED ($>$80\%) because cuDNN's
internal kernels carry no NVTX annotations and no Inductor debug metadata.  Bottleneck diagnosis
in this case proceeds by \emph{kernel-name inference}: the cuDNN FP32 SIMT path
(\texttt{Kernel2}) is identifiable by name; BF16 inputs route cuDNN to a Tensor Core path
(\texttt{sm80\_xmma\_gemm\_bf16bf16\_*} on A100).  This workload demonstrates that \sys's
closed-loop protocol applies even when hardware counters are unavailable: the profiler identifies
the cuDNN bottleneck class, kernel-name inference suggests the fix, and re-profiling confirms the

\subsection{DepthwiseSeparableConv}

The DepthwiseSepConv workload provides a textbook roofline contrast: depthwise convolutions
(groups=C\textsubscript{in}, 3$\times$3 kernel) are pure memory-bandwidth-bound with arithmetic
intensity $\approx$0.3\,FLOP/byte, far left of the ridge point; pointwise convolutions (1$\times$1)
are equivalent to batched GEMMs and are compute-bound with Tensor Core engagement when using BF16
inputs.  Within a single block, both extremes of the roofline co-exist, making this workload useful
for verifying that \sys's bottleneck taxonomy correctly classifies each operator independently.
